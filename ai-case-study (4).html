01 · The Problem

Research at scale has expensive blind spots.

Before AI, two friction points slowed everything down and created costly rework downstream.

Friction Point 01

Missing products found too late

  • Product gaps were discovered during or after testing — when retests are expensive.
  • Market scanning depended on whoever had bandwidth to read, so coverage was deep but narrow.
  • A single missed product could invalidate a review's credibility with search algorithms.
Friction Point 02

Test plans with no shared anchor

  • Test plans were informal and varied based on who was writing them.
  • No documented, consumer-grounded framework the whole team worked from.
  • Reviews sometimes skewed toward technical depth over what readers actually care about.
02 · The Solution

Three prompts that changed how we work.

The intervention was simple: structured AI prompts at the start of each research cycle. Each prompt targets a specific failure point in the old pipeline.

Prompt 01 Market Landscape
What's actually out there in this category?
What are the leading [product category] products on the market right now? Include mainstream options, newer entrants, and any products with notable consumer buzz or search presence. Flag anything we might be overlooking.

Closes the bandwidth gap on market coverage. Instead of relying on whoever had time to read, we get a broad sweep in minutes — then apply expert judgment to what matters.

Prompt 02 Product Lineup & SEO
What belongs in this lineup — and what's just taking up space?
What are the most important products that need to be included in a top-of-search ranking review? Evaluate the current lineup of [category] and tell me what products are still current and which are outdated. Please include some potential award ideas along with the product list and evaluate what could be done to improve search rank and traffic: [URL]

This prompt does double duty: it validates the lineup before purchasing and flags gaps in our SEO footprint. It's the one that most directly prevents the costly retest scenario.

Prompt 03 Consumer Purchase Criteria
What does someone actually need to know before they buy?
What are the most critical factors for a consumer to know before they purchase [product] and what are the key purchase decisions?

Grounds the test plan in what readers are actually trying to figure out — not what we find technically interesting. Every data point we collect should answer a question someone is genuinely asking.

04 · Results

What actually changed.

The gains weren't abstract. Each one tied directly to a friction point that was slowing us down or creating noise downstream.

🎯
Earlier, Sharper Product Inclusion

Critical products are flagged before the test phase begins — eliminating the retest scenarios that were the biggest source of pipeline slowdown.

📋
Test Plans That Actually Hold Together

Every purchase decision is now accounted for before testing starts. The plan functions as a foundation, not an afterthought — and it carries through to the writing phase.

🤝
Team Cohesion Around a Shared Document

A documented test plan gives everyone a common reference point. Less ambiguity, fewer disconnects between testers and writers.

05 · Implications

What this tells us about AI in editorial work.

The most interesting finding wasn't efficiency. It was what the AI reflected back about what we were already doing — and what we'd quietly drifted from.

I
AI as a mirror, not just a tool

A lot of what the AI recommended mirrored strategy we'd already implemented — and reminded us where we'd quietly drifted from it. I'd felt for a while that our reviews were getting too dense: too many charts, too much data-driven jargon. The data validated our credibility, but it wasn't always the most useful output for a busy consumer trying to make a purchase decision.

The right answer is a balance — objective numbers paired with clear writing that explains why those numbers matter to the reader. Using Prompt 02 in particular, the whole team got a concrete reminder of that nuance and collectively refocused.

II
The balancing act of product selection

In broad product categories, the lineup decision is where everything downstream gets determined. Miss a product someone is searching for and you bounce them off the page. Include too many and you bloat the testing and writing process — both of which are real costs.

The AI analysis provides guard rails without being prescriptive. It captures the items we need while steering us away from products that would dilute the review without adding value. The judgment call still belongs to the team — but now we're making it with better information, earlier.
